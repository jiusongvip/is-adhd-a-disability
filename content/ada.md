 # Is ADHD a Disability Under the ADA?
 
 The short answer is **yes**. ADHD can qualify as a disability under the ADA when it substantially limits one or more major life activities. Here is how the law defines it and what protections you have.
 
 ## What the ADA says about disability
 
 The Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990 (ADA) defines a person with a disability as someone who:
 
 1. Has a physical or mental impairment that **substantially limits** one or more major life activities
 2. Has a record of such an impairment, or
 3. Is regarded as having such an impairment
 
 ADHD falls under "mental impairment" and can qualify under any of these three definitions. Most ADHD-related ADA claims rely on the first definition.
 
 ## Major life activities affected by ADHD
 
 - **Concentrating and thinking** -- the most commonly cited limitation for ADHD
 - **Working** -- when ADHD impairs job performance
 - **Learning and reading** -- especially relevant in educational settings
 - **Communicating** -- including social interactions affected by impulsivity
 - **Sleeping** -- recognized as a major life activity since 2008
 
 ## The ADAAA of 2008: a critical shift
 
 The **ADA Amendments Act of 2008 (ADAAA)** significantly expanded protections for people with ADHD. The ADAAA:
 
 - Broadened the definition of "substantially limits"
 - Clarified that **mitigating measures** (such as medication) cannot be considered when determining disability status
 - Stated that an impairment that is episodic or in remission can still be a disability
 - Shifted focus from whether someone is disabled to whether discrimination occurred
 
 This means: even if you take ADHD medication and function well, your ADHD can still qualify as a disability under the ADA.
 
 ## Employer obligations under the ADA
 
 Under the ADA, employers with 15 or more employees must:
 
 - Provide reasonable accommodations to qualified employees with disabilities
 - Not discriminate in hiring, firing, promotion, or compensation based on disability
 - Engage in an "interactive process" to determine appropriate accommodations
 - Not retaliate against employees who request accommodations
 
 ## Key court cases
 
 - **Jacques v. DiMarzio, Inc. (2002):** Employee with ADHD received $320,000 for wrongful termination after employer failed to accommodate.
 - **Gagliardo v. Connaught Laboratories (2002):** Third Circuit found ADHD to be a compensable disability under the ADA.
 - **E.E.O.C. v. United Parcel Service (2008):** Settled for failure to engage in the interactive process for an employee with ADHD.
 
 ## How to request ADA accommodations
 
 1. Obtain documentation from your healthcare provider describing your ADHD diagnosis and specific limitations.
 2. Identify the accommodations that would help you perform your job.
 3. Submit a written request to your employer's HR department.
 4. Participate in the interactive process to find a mutually acceptable solution.
 5. If denied, you can file a complaint with the **EEOC** (Equal Employment Opportunity Commission).
 
 > **Do I need to disclose my ADHD to my employer?**
 > No. You are not required to disclose your ADHD to your employer. However, if you want ADA protections and accommodations, you must disclose your disability and request accommodations. Your employer is not required to provide accommodations you never asked for.
 
 ## Limitations of ADA protection
 
 The ADA does not cover all situations:
 
 - Employers with fewer than 15 employees (though state laws may apply)
 - If the accommodation would cause "undue hardship" (significant difficulty or expense)
 - If the employee cannot perform the essential functions of the job even with accommodations
 - If the employee poses a direct threat to health or safety
