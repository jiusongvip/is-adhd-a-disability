 # Can You Get Disability Benefits for ADHD? SSDI and SSI Guide
 
 Yes, it is possible to receive SSDI or SSI benefits for ADHD, but the bar is high. The Social Security Administration requires strong evidence that ADHD severely limits your ability to work. Here is what you need to know.
 
 ## SSDI vs. SSI: what is the difference?
 
 | Feature | SSDI | SSI |
 |---|---|---|
 | Based on | Work credits and payroll taxes paid | Financial need (limited income and assets) |
 | Medical requirement | Same: disability prevents substantial gainful activity | Same |
 | Health insurance | Medicare after 24 months | Medicaid (in most states) |
 
 ## The SSA listing for ADHD
 
 The SSA evaluates ADHD under Listing 112.11 (for children) and in adult claims under mental disorder listings. For adults, ADHD is typically evaluated under **Listing 12.11** (neurodevelopmental disorders). To qualify, you must demonstrate:
 
 - **Medical documentation** of ADHD diagnosis with symptoms of hyperactivity, impulsivity, and/or inattention
 - **Marked limitation** in at least two of: understanding and applying information, interacting with others, concentrating and maintaining pace, adapting and managing oneself
 - Or the ADHD must be "serious and persistent" with a documented history over at least 2 years
 
 ## Challenges: why ADHD-only claims are difficult
 
 The SSA often denies ADHD-only claims because:
 
 - Many people with ADHD hold jobs and function in daily life
 - ADHD symptoms can be managed with medication and coping strategies
 - Documentation requirements are stringent and the condition is subjective
 - Symptoms often fluctuate, making "consistent and severe" hard to prove
 
 Claims are more likely to succeed when ADHD is combined with other diagnosed conditions such as anxiety, depression, learning disabilities, or other mental health conditions.
 
 ## How to strengthen your claim
 
 1. **Comprehensive medical records:** Include physician notes, neuropsychological testing results, medication history, therapy notes.
 2. **Work history documentation:** Performance reviews, disciplinary records, job termination letters that show ADHD-related work difficulties.
 3. **Function report:** A detailed account of how ADHD affects your daily activities and ability to work.
 4. **Third-party statements:** Letters from supervisors, coworkers, or family describing your limitations.
 5. **RFC assessment:** A Residual Functional Capacity evaluation from your treating physician.
 6. **Legal representation:** SSDI attorneys work on contingency and significantly improve approval odds.
 
 > **Approval rates and what to expect:** About 67% of initial SSDI applications are denied. Most approvals come at the Administrative Law Judge hearing stage. Be prepared for a process that can take 12-24 months. Do not give up after the first denial.
