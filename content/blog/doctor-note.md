 # How to Get an ADHD Disability Doctor's Note
 
 A well-written doctor's note is often the cornerstone of any ADHD disability claim -- whether for workplace accommodations, school services, or SSDI benefits. Here is how to get one that actually helps.
 
 ## Why the right documentation matters
 
 A generic note that says "John has ADHD" is not enough. For disability claims, employers, schools, and the SSA need specific information about **functional limitations** -- how your ADHD actually affects your ability to work, learn, or manage daily life.
 
 ## What a strong doctor's note includes
 
 1. **Provider credentials:** The doctor's license number, specialty, and contact information.
 2. **Diagnosis:** Formal ADHD diagnosis with date and diagnostic criteria used (e.g., DSM-5).
 3. **Treatment history:** Medications prescribed, therapy, and response to treatment.
 4. **Functional limitations:** Specific ways ADHD affects daily functioning -- not just "has trouble concentrating" but "cannot complete multi-step tasks without written instructions and frequent check-ins."
 5. **Recommended accommodations:** Specific, practical suggestions.
 6. **Duration and prognosis:** How long the condition is expected to last and expected outcomes.
 
 ## How to talk to your doctor about it
 
 Many doctors are unfamiliar with writing disability documentation for ADHD. Come prepared:
 
 - Bring a **specific list** of your work or school challenges.
 - Bring a copy of your **job description** or academic requirements.
 - Bring a list of **accommodations you think would help**.
 - Explain **why the documentation is needed** (HR request, 504 evaluation, SSDI application).
 - If the doctor is hesitant, ask them to document your **observed symptoms and functional limitations** rather than making legal conclusions.
 
 ## Sample letter structure
 
 While not a template to copy, here is what a strong letter looks like structurally:
 
 > [Provider letterhead]
 >
 > To Whom It May Concern:
 >
 > [Patient name] has been under my care since [date] for Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD), diagnosed per DSM-5 criteria. Despite ongoing treatment including [medication/therapy], [patient] continues to experience the following functional limitations: [specific examples].
 >
 > I recommend the following accommodations to address these limitations: [list]. These accommodations are medically necessary to allow [patient] to perform the essential functions of [their job / their education].
 
 ## What if your doctor refuses?
 
 Some doctors are reluctant to write disability documentation, especially for ADHD. If this happens:
 
 - **Ask why.** Understand their concern -- is it about the specific accommodation requested, or documentation in general?
 - **Seek a second opinion.** A psychiatrist or clinical psychologist who specializes in ADHD may be more familiar with disability documentation.
 - **Get a neuropsychological evaluation.** A formal evaluation provides objective evidence of functional limitations.
 - **Remember:** Your employer or school cannot require you to see a specific doctor.
