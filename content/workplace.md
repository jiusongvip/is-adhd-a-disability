 # Is ADHD a Disability at Work?
 
 Yes. ADHD is recognized as a disability in the workplace under the ADA, giving you the right to reasonable accommodations and protection from discrimination. The key question is not *whether* ADHD qualifies, but *practically how* to navigate workplace rights.
 
 ## Your rights under the ADA at work
 
 Under the ADA, employees with ADHD who work for employers with 15 or more employees have the right to:
 
 - **Reasonable accommodations** that enable you to perform essential job functions
 - **Freedom from discrimination** in hiring, firing, promotion, compensation, and all other terms of employment
 - **Confidentiality** -- your employer must keep your disability information separate from your personnel file
 - **Protection from retaliation** for requesting accommodations or filing complaints
 
 ## The disclosure decision
 
 One of the hardest decisions for people with ADHD is whether to disclose their condition at work.
 
 ### Reasons to disclose
 
 - You need specific accommodations to perform well
 - Your performance issues might otherwise be misinterpreted as laziness or incompetence
 - You want to build an open, trusting relationship with your manager
 - You want to help reduce stigma around neurodivergence
 
 ### Reasons to be cautious
 
 - Stigma and misconceptions about ADHD persist in many workplaces
 - Disclosure may affect how colleagues and managers perceive your capabilities
 - Not all employers follow the law -- illegal discrimination can be hard to prove
 - You may not yet know what accommodations would help
 
 > **A practical approach:** Many people with ADHD find a middle path: do not disclose during the hiring process, but if performance issues arise or specific accommodations are needed after you have established yourself, make a formal request through HR with supporting medical documentation. You are not required to tell your direct manager your specific diagnosis -- only that you have a qualifying disability and need accommodations.
 
 ## Common workplace accommodations for ADHD
 
 - **Flexible schedule:** Adjusted start/end times or remote work options
 - **Quiet workspace:** Private office, noise-canceling headphones, or permission to work in a quiet area
 - **Structured task management:** Written instructions, checklists, project management tools
 - **Frequent check-ins:** Regular one-on-one meetings with supervisor to clarify priorities
 - **Minimized distractions:** Permission to turn off email notifications, use focus apps, block calendar time
 - **Assistive technology:** Speech-to-text software, time management apps, note-taking tools
 - **Modified training:** Hands-on training instead of long written manuals
 
 ## What to do if your request is denied
 
 1. **Ask for the reason in writing.** Employers must provide a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason.
 2. **Propose alternatives.** If one accommodation is denied, suggest other options that achieve the same outcome.
 3. **Document everything.** Keep records of all communications about your request.
 4. **File a complaint with the EEOC.** The Equal Employment Opportunity Commission handles ADA discrimination complaints. You typically have 180 days from the date of discrimination to file.
 5. **Consult an employment attorney.** If you believe you have been discriminated against, legal counsel can help evaluate your case.
 
 **Disclaimer:** This page provides general information about workplace rights, not legal advice. Employment law is complex and varies by jurisdiction. Consult with an employment attorney for guidance specific to your situation.
